Lesson 10 : Signs of Opioid Overuse or Overdose

Anyone using opioids is at risk of overmedicine or overdose if they take too much. You are much more at risk of overmedicine or overdose if you have never taken opioids. Someone who has never taken an opioid may experience stronger effects of the drugs and worse side effects of opioids because their bodies are not used to them.

Some signs of overmedicine include:

- Slurred speech, stumbling while walking, dizziness, or confusion
- Excessive drowsiness or difficulty staying alert
- Difficulty waking from sleep

Some signs of overdose include:

- Cannot stay awake or, if awake, is unable to speak or be kept awake
- Trouble breathing, including slow, shallow breathing or periods in which breathing stops
- Limpness, lifelessness
- Pale or clammy skin, or blue fingernails or lips
- Slow or stopped heartbeat

What should I do if someone has taken too much medicine?

Call 911 Right Away!

Give the operator as much information as possible, including what opioid you think they may have taken and when it was taken.

Naloxone (brand name: Narcan) is a medicine that blocks the effects of opioids.

It quickly reverses the breathing problems that result from an opioid overdose. Naloxone can be given as a nasal spray or by injection to a person who has overdosed. Naloxone is available in most U.S. states without a prescription. Providers may also prescribe it along with an opioid medicine. You can get it at your local pharmacy. Family and friends of people taking opioids are encouraged to have naloxone at home so it can be given in the case of a suspected overdose. If naloxone is available, administer it. However, it is important to understand that the reversal of an opioid overdose with naloxone may only be temporary. The person who has overdosed will still need medical attention.

If the person is having trouble breathing, the 911 operator may ask if you are trained in cardiopulmonary resuscitation(CPR). If so, you might be asked to perform CPR.

Stay with the person until emergency help arrives, even if the person wakes up.

Treating an Opioid Addiction

Anyone suspected of taking an overdose of opioids should see a medical provider right away. This is true even if the overdose may have been accidental. A health professional can diagnose opioid use disorder (misusing opioids) and opioid addiction. Opioid addiction is a chronic illness and should be treated the same as other chronic illnesses. Treatment for opioid addiction is different for each person.

The main goal of treatment is to help you stop using the drug. Treatment also can help you avoid using it again in the future. When you stop using opioids, your body will react. You will have a number of symptoms that may include nausea and vomiting, stomach pain, and anxiety. This reaction is called withdrawal. Your healthcare provider can prescribe certain medicines to help relieve your withdrawal symptoms. They also will help control your cravings.

Lesson 10 : Quiz Questions!

1. True or False: Someone who has never taken opioids before is at higher risk of overdose.

2. True or False: Someone who has overdosed on opioids may be difficult to wake up.

3. True or False: There is nothing that can reverse and opioid overdose.

4. True or False: The first thing to do when you suspect an opioid overdose is to call 911.

5. True or False: Anyone who has had an opioid overdose should be evaluated for opioid use disorder.

Quiz Answers

Question 1: True - Someone who has never taken an opioid may experience stronger effects of the drugs and worse side effects of opioids because their bodies are not used to them.

Question 2: True - Opioids can make you feel sleepy. When a person takes too high a dose, they may appear to be asleep and cannot be woken.

Question 3: False - Naloxone (brand name: Narcan) is a medicine that blocks the effects of opioids. It quickly reverses the breathing problems that result from an opioid overdose. Naloxone can be given as a nasal spray or by injection to a person who has overdosed.

Question 4: True - The person who has overdosed will need medical attention. Even though naloxone can reverse an overdose, it may only a temporary effect.

Question 5: True - An overdose could be accidental. However, anyone who has overdosed on an opioid should be seen by a licensed healthcare provider for further evaluation. If the overdose was due to opioid addition, there are treatments that can help the person safely stop taking opioids.
